\section{Methods}

There are varying definitions and conventions in the study of time series data and stochastic processes more generally. To simplify our presentation, this work closely follows the \citet{hansen_econometrics_2022} \emph{Econometrics} textbook as a primary reference. Throughout, we will frequently refer the reader to specific results from this textbook.

\subsection{Assumptions} \label{sec:assumptions}

Let $\{X_t\}_{t\in\{\dots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \dots\}}$ be a discrete-time stochastic process that is \nameref{sec:weak-stationarity} and \nameref{sec:strong-mixing}, and let $x = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T\}$ where $x_t$ denotes an observed sample of $X_t$. For simplicity, assume $\mathbb{E}[X_t] = 0$. 

\subsubsection{Weakly stationary} \label{sec:weak-stationarity}
Weak stationarity implies a constant mean and variance (independent of time index $t$), and an autocovariance function that only depends on time lag $k$, $\gamma_k = \text{cov}[X_t, X_{t-k}] =\mathbb{E}[X_t X_{t-k}]$. For analysis, we use a normalized measure of the autocovariances, the \textit{autocorrelation function (ACF)}:
\begin{align} \label{eq:acf}
\rho_k = \text{corr}(X_t, X_{t-k}) = \gamma_k/\gamma_0,
\end{align}

\noindent where $\gamma_k$ is the autocovariance at lag $k$ and $\gamma_0$ is the variance. As a correlation, $|\rho_k|\le 1$, but stationarity alone does not guarantee decay of $\rho_k$ with increasing lag; constant or periodic processes can maintain nonzero correlations indefinitely. 

\subsubsection{Strong Mixing} \label{sec:strong-mixing}
Strong mixing ($\alpha$-mixing) imposes stronger dependence constraints than stationarity while still allowing for a wide set of stochastic processes. By definition, a process is strong mixing if $\alpha(\ell) \rightarrow 0$ as $\ell \rightarrow \infty$, where $\alpha(\ell)$ measures the \emph{maximum dependence} between events separated by $\ell$ time points; this controls the full joint dependence structure and implies distant events are uncorrelated. Strong mixing also implies ergodicity \citep[Chapter~14.12]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, which ensures consistent estimation by the ergodic theorem \citep[Theorem~14.9]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. Additionally, if a mixing process has $r>2$ finite moments $\mathbb{E}|X_t|^r < \infty$ and its mixing coefficients satisfy $\sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty} \alpha(\ell)^{1-2/r} < \infty$, then its autocorrelations decay sufficiently fast for application of the central limit theorem for dependent data \citep[Theorem~14.15]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. These conditions enable asymptotic theory for estimation and inference on timescale maps (see \nameref{sec:estimator-properties}).\\

As introduced by \citet{murray_hierarchy_2014}, the timescale $\tau$ represents the lag where exponentially decaying autocorrelations reach $1/e \approx 0.37$, analogous to the time constants of many physical systems and often referred to as $e$-folding time in other fields. While it provides an intuitive description of the memory or persistence of that process, assuming an exponential function imposes stricter constraints than strong mixing, which alone does not prescribe any specific type of decay (exponential, linear, damped periodic, etc.). This highlights an important distinction between the data-generating process and the simplified parametric model used to describe the timescale at which such a process becomes decorrelated. In the present paper, we adopt broad assumptions, requiring only that the process is stationary and mixing, to account for cases where the ACF decay may not be strictly exponential.

\subsubsection{Estimation and Inference under Misspecification}

We approximate the dominant exponential decay in autocorrelations by a single timescale parameter $\tau$, and formally evaluate two timescale methods that are commonly applied across neuroimaging modalities (fMRI, EEG, ECoG, MEG). The time-domain (TD) linear model estimated with linear least squares \citep{kaneoke_variance_2012, meisel_decline_2017, huang_timescales_2018, lurie_cortical_2024, shinn_functional_2023, shafiei_topographic_2020, spitmaan_multiple_2020, trepka_training-dependent_2024}, and the autocorrelation-domain (AD) nonlinear model estimated with nonlinear least squares \citep{rossi-pool_invariant_2021, cirillo_neural_2018, ito_cortical_2020, runyan_distinct_2017, zeraati_flexible_2022, nougaret_intrinsic_2021, wasmuht_intrinsic_2018, muller_core_2020, maisson_choice-relevant_2021, li_hierarchical_2022, shafiei_topographic_2020}. Both belong to the subclass of m-estimators defined by by minimizing a sample loss function using squared error loss. This approach seeks the best approximation in terms of squared error and does not require full specification of the data-generating distribution, relying on method of moments instead of maximum likelihood estimation. \\

Throughout, a superscript star (e.g. $\tau^*$) denotes the pseudo-true parameter corresponding to the best fitting parameter in a possibly misspecified model. If correctly specified, $\tau^*$ is the true timescale; otherwise, it is an approximation. Acknowledging that the data-generating process and fitted model will likely be different in practice, we introduce standard errors that account for this mismatch, enabling valid inference despite misspecification of the autocorrelation decay. Under the above assumptions and relevant moment conditions, the m-estimators are consistent for the pseudo-true parameter and asymptotically normal (see \nameref{sec:estimator-properties}). Further, they are invariant under the reparameterization needed to define timescales, with asymptotic normality preserved by the delta method, ensuring valid inference for transformed parameters.

\subsection{Timescale Definitions}

\subsubsection{Time-Domain Linear Model}\label{sec:time-domain-linear-model}
A first order autoregressive model (AR1) provides a linear approximation of timescale. The AR1 model:
\begin{align}\label{eq:ar1}
    X_t = \phitd X_{t-1} + e_t,
\end{align}

\noindent models the process as a linear regression between $X_t$ and $X_{t-1}$ in the time domain with \textit{iid} innovations $e_t$. In the autocorrelation domain, it implies that the theoretical ACF decays exponentially at a rate determined by $\phitd$, such that $\rho_k = \phitd^k$ \citep[Chapter~14.22]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. For a stationary process with $|\phitd|<1$, the exponential decay rate can be directly obtained from $\phitd$, with a timescale $\tautd$ equal to the lag at which the AR1-projected ACF reaches $1/e\approx 0.37$, that is,  $\rho_\tau = \phitd^\tau = 1/e$, resulting in $\tautd = g(\phitd) = -1/\text{log}(|\phitd|)$. The timescale $\tautd$ is expressed as a nonlinear function of $\phitd$, denoted by $g(\phitd)$. This defines $\tautd$ to be a real number even though the ACF only includes integer indices. The absolute value allows for $\phitd<0$ and is introduced as a modeling assumption to ensure the timescale captures the monotonic decay rate of autocorrelations, consistent with the approach of \citet{murray_hierarchy_2014}.\\

The parameter $\phitd^*$ is the value that minimizes the expected squared error function $S(\phitd)$:
\begin{align}
    S(\phitd) = \mathbb{E}[(X_t - \phitd X_{t-1})^2],\quad \phitd^* = \underset{\phitd}{\text{argmin}} \; S(\phitd).
\end{align}

\noindent $S(\phitd)$ is minimized by taking its derivative with respect to $\phitd$, setting it to zero, and solving for $\phitd^*$:
\begin{align}
    \frac{d}{d\phitd} S(\phitd) = -2 \mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}(X_t - \phitd X_{t-1})] = 0.
\end{align}

\noindent Differentiating the quadratic function yields a linear equation in $\phitd$, and solving this results in a closed-form expression for the optimal $\phitd^*$. Therefore, $\phitd^*$ is defined by \textit{linear projection} and the timescale parameter $\tautd^*$ by a change of variable:
\begin{align}
    \phitd^* &= (\mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2])^{-1}(\mathbb{E}[X_t X_{t-1}]) \label{eq:ar1-phi},\\
    \tautd^* &= g(\phitd^*) = -\frac{1}{\text{log}(|\phitd^*|)}. \label{eq:ar1-tau}
\end{align}

\noindent Thus, the timescale represents the characteristic time of $1/e$ autocorrelation decay derived from the best-fitting AR1 model under squared error loss. Since $X_t$ is stationary with finite variance, the parameters $\phitd^*$ and $\tautd^*$ defined by projection are unique; in fact, any approximating AR1 model is identifiable if $\mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2]$ is nonzero and finite \citep[Theorem~14.28]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. Importantly, the observed process $X_t$ need not follow the AR1 model from equation~\eqref{eq:ar1} with \textit{iid} innovations $e_t$. This allows for projection errors, i.e. residuals from linear projection onto the working model, which may exhibit unequal variance and autocorrelation. This applies to any stationary and mixing process, even if the true data-generating process is not AR1, so the resulting fit an AR1 projection.

\begin{align}
    e_t^* = X_t - \phitd^* X_{t-1}, \quad \sigmatd^{*2} = \mathbb{E}[e_t^{*2}]
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation-Domain Nonlinear Model}\label{sec:autocorrelation-domain-nonlinear-model}

Alternatively, timescales can be defined in the autocorrelation domain by an exponential decay function, as introduced by \citet{murray_hierarchy_2014}. For consistent notation, we write the autocorrelation-domain nonlinear model as:
\begin{align}\label{eq:nlm}
    \rho_k = \phiad^k + e_k, \; \text{for}\; k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, K\},
\end{align}

\noindent where $\rho_k$ denotes the autocorrelation at lag $k$ and $e_k$ captures any structure in the ACF not explained by the single-parameter exponential decay $\phiad^k$. This allows the ACF to exhibit other behaviors consistent with general stationary and mixing conditions, such as multi-exponential decay, damped periodic oscillations, or other complex temporal dependencies. The relationship between $\rho_k$ and $k$ is nonlinear in $\phiad$ which determines the exponential decay rate. Unlike the \nameref{sec:time-domain-linear-model} above, this definition captures exponential decay across multiple ($K$) lags of the ACF rather than by a single lag, capturing longer-range temporal dependencies. Here, $k$ is a deterministic lag index and all statements below average across this index rather than condition on a single lag. Consequently, the parameter $\phiad$ here is not the same as the time-domain parameter $\phitd$, since both its value and interpretation differ when influenced by multiple lags.\\

Here, the projection parameter $\phiad^*$ is the value that minimizes the expected squared error function $S(\phiad)$:
\begin{align}\label{eq:nlm_loss}
    S(\phiad) = \mathbb{E}[(\rho_k - \phiad^k)^2], \quad \phiad^* = \underset{\phiad}{\text{argmin}} \; S(\phiad).
\end{align}

\noindent The expectation denotes a function of population expectations aggregated across lags. $S(\phiad)$ is minimized by taking its derivative with respect to $\phiad$, setting it to zero, and solving for $\phiad$:
\begin{align}
    \frac{d}{d\phiad} S(\phiad) &= -2\mathbb{E}[(k\phiad^{k-1})(\rho_k - \phiad^k)] = 0.
\end{align}

\noindent However, the derivative is nonlinear in $\phiad$, preventing a closed-form solution for least squares minimization. This is because the first-order condition sums nonlinear terms across many lags, yielding a high-order equation with no general algebraic solution. Therefore, optimization methods are needed to approximate $\phiad^*$ by \textit{nonlinear projection} of the lag-indexed ACF onto the curve $\phiad^k$ under the squared error loss in \eqref{eq:nlm_loss}. Like before, the corresponding timescale is defined as the time lag at which the fitted ACF reaches $1/e$ and can be expressed by the change or variable:
\begin{align}
    \tauad^* &= g(\phiad^*) = -\frac{1}{\text{log}(|\phiad^*|)}. \label{eq:nlm-tau}
\end{align}

The pseudo-true parameter $\phiad$ is the nonlinear least-squares approximation of the ACF by a single exponential, with projection error residuals $e_k^* = \rho_k - \phiad$. These errors reduce to zero when the data-generating process is AR1, otherwise they are a function of lags $k$ that absorb the non-exponential or damped-oscillatory features allowed under stationary and mixing. 

\subsection{Timescale Estimation}\label{sec:timescale-estimation}
\subsubsection{Time-Domain Linear Estimator}\label{sec:time-domain-linear-estimator}
Given observations $x_1, \ldots, x_T$ are demeaned, the linear least squares estimator of the \nameref{sec:time-domain-linear-model} is obtained by replacing the expectations in equation~\eqref{eq:ar1-phi} using method of moments. It has the following closed-form expression:
\begin{align}
    \hatphitd &= \left(\sum_{t=2}^{T+1} x_{t-1}^2\right)^{-1} \left(\sum_{t=2}^T x_t x_{t-1}\right),\\
    \hattautd &= g(\hatphitd) = -\frac{1}{\text{log}(|\hatphitd|)},
\end{align}

\noindent where $\hatphitd$ and $\hattautd$ are the sample versions of the population parameters from equations~\eqref{eq:ar1-phi} and \eqref{eq:ar1-tau}, respectively \citep[Chapter~14.3]{hansen_econometrics_2022}.\\


\subsubsection{Autocorrelation-Domain Nonlinear Estimator}\label{sec:autocorrelation-domain-nonlinear-estimator}
The nonlinear least squares estimator of the \nameref{sec:autocorrelation-domain-nonlinear-model} is fit to the ACF, so the time series needs to be first transformed into the autocorrelation domain. For a finite-length and centered time series, the population ACF from equation~\eqref{eq:acf} is estimated by:
\begin{align}\label{eq:acf_}
    \hat\rho_k &= \hat\gamma_0^{-1}\hat\gamma_k = \left(\sum_{t=1}^T x_t^2\right)^{-1} \left(\sum_{t=k+1}^{T}x_t x_{t-k}\right),
\end{align}

\noindent where $\hat\gamma_k$ is the sample covariance at lag $k$ and $\hat\gamma_0$ is the sample variance. Under the mixing assumption, the population ACF \eqref{eq:acf} approaches zero as lag $k$ increases. However, sampling variability may yield non-zero autocorrelations even when true values are zero. To mitigate this, the sample ACF estimator \eqref{eq:acf_} imposes a bias towards zero by scaling the autocovariances ($\hat \gamma_k$, calculated using $T-k$ terms) by the total sample variance ($\hat\gamma_0$, calculated using all $T$ timepoints). The finite sample performance of this ACF estimator was compared to other standard estimators and shown to perform best in terms of bias and variance \citep{marriott_bias_1954}.\\

By the model definition \eqref{eq:nlm}, the exponential decay parameter $\phiad^*$ that minimizes the cost function, $S(\phiad)$ in equation~\eqref{eq:nlm_loss}, is estimated by minimizing the sample analog $\widehat{S}_{K}(\phiad)$:
\begin{align}
    \widehat{S}_{K}(\phiad) &= \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=0}^K (\hat\rho_k - \phiad^k)^2, \label{eq:nls_loss_}\\
    \hatphiad &= \underset{\phiad}{\text{argmin}} \; \widehat{S}_{K}(\phiad), \label{eq:nls_phi_}\\
    \hattauad &= g(\hatphiad) = -\frac{1}{\text{log}(|\hatphiad|)}.
\end{align}

\noindent In this paper we use the Levenberg-Marquart algorithm to iteratively update the estimate of $\hatphiad$ until convergence (i.e., when the step size goes below a $10^{-6}$ tolerance) \citep{levenberg_method_1944}.\\

\subsection{Standard Error of the Estimators}\label{sec:stderr}
\subsubsection{Time-Domain Standard Error}\label{sec:stderr-time-domain}
We provide a theoretical standard error expression for the estimator $\hattautd$ under model misspecification. When the data-generating process deviates from AR1 such that the errors are dependent and/or heteroskedastic, the usual (naive) standard errors will be biased. As a result, confidence intervals or hypothesis tests that rely on them are invalid. To correct for this, the \citet{newey_simple_1987} (NW) expression takes a sandwich form and explicitly accounts for misspecification by summing the covariance structure of the errors, ensuring that the resulting standard errors are asymptotically valid \citep[Theorem 14.32]{hansen_econometrics_2022}.\\

Given that $X_t$ is stationary and mixing, so too are the errors from equation~\eqref{eq:ar1} since these properties are preserved by finite transformations \citep[Theorem~14.2 and Theorem~14.12]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. Consequently, the autocovariances of the errors vanish as the time lag increases (see \nameref{sec:assumptions}). Further, because the timescale estimator $\hattautd$ is given by the nonlinear function $g(\hatphitd)$, its theoretical standard error can be approximated by the delta method, which uses the derivative with respect to $\phitd$ evaluated at the true parameter value $\phitd^*$:
\begin{align}
    \text{se}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphitd) = \sqrt{\qtd^{-1}\; \omegatd \; \qtd^{-1}} \label{eq:se-ar1-phi}, \quad
    \text{se}_{\text{NW}}(\hattautd) \approx \text{se}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphitd) \cdot \frac{d}{d\phitd} g(\phitd)\big|_{\phitd=\phitd^*},
\end{align}

\noindent where
\begin{align}
    \qtd = \mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2] \quad\text{and}\quad \omegatd = \sum_{\ell=-\infty}^{\infty} \mathbb{E}[(X_{t-1} \cdot e_t^*)(X_{t-1-\ell} \cdot e_{t-\ell}^*)].
\end{align}

\noindent These moments, $\qtd$ and $\omegatd$, are stationary and therefore do not depend on time index $t$. The covariance terms in $\omegatd$ capture deviations in the error structure from the standard \textit{iid} case. For the special case of correct specification, when $X_t$ is a true AR1 process, the standard error of $\hatphitd$ reduces to the usual formula:
\begin{align}
    \text{se}_{\text{Naive}}(\hatphitd) = \sqrt{\sigmatd^2 \qtd^{-1}},
\end{align}

\noindent where $\sigmatd^2$ is the time-domain error variance. \\

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation-Domain Standard Error}\label{sec:stderr-autocorrelation-domain}
Equation~\eqref{eq:nlm} models autocorrelation decay using a parametric exponential function. While usual (naive) applications assume \textit{iid} errors, our approach permits dependent errors generated by an underlying stationary and mixing process. The standard errors proposed below account for potential misspecification of the exponential form and are asymptotically valid under these more general conditions.\\

Following the description in \citet[Chapter~22.8 and Chapter~23.5]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, if $\phiad^*$ uniquely minimizes $S(\phiad)$ in equation~\eqref{eq:nlm_loss}, such that $S(\phiad) > S(\phiad^*)$ for all $\phiad \neq \phiad^*$, the theoretical standard error of the estimator $\hatphiad$ can be computed using a \citet{newey_simple_1987} (NW) form that reflects both the curvature of the squared loss function at its minimum and the covariance of the errors. Further, because the timescale estimator $\hattauad$ is given by the nonlinear function $g(\hatphiad)$, its theoretical standard error can be approximated by the delta method using the derivative with respect to $\phiad$ evaluated at the true parameter value $\phiad^*$:
\begin{align}\label{eq:stderr-autocorrelation-domain}
    \text{se}_\text{NW}(\hatphiad) = \sqrt{\qad^{-1}\; \omegaad \;\qad^{-1}}, \quad \text{se}_\text{NW}(\hattauad) \approx \text{se}_\text{NW}(\hatphiad) \cdot \frac{d}{d\phiad}g(\phiad)\big|_{\phiad=\phiad^*}.
\end{align}

\noindent The components $\qad$ and $\omegaad$ are population quantities derived from the regression function $m(k, \phiad) = \phiad^k$ in \eqref{eq:nlm} and its derivative $m_{\phiad, k} = \frac{d}{d\phiad} m(k, \phiad) = k \phiad^{k-1}$, defined and evaluated at the minimizer $\phiad^*$ as:
\begin{align}
    \qad = \mathbb{E}[m_{\phiad^*, k}^2] = \mathbb{E}[(k \phiad^{*k-1})^2] \quad\text{and}\quad
    \omegaad = \sum_{\ell=-\infty}^{\infty} \mathbb{E}[(m_{\phiad^*, k} \cdot e_{k})(m_{\phiad^*, k-\ell} \cdot e_{k-\ell})].
\end{align}

\noindent The derivative of the regression function $m_{\phiad^*, k}$ evaluated at $\phiad^*$ locally approximates the nonlinear model by a linear one, and the expression for $\omegaad$ sums the covariance structure of the errors, ensuring that the standard errors are asymptotically valid even under model misspecification (see \eqref{eq:nls-phi-clt}). This is a realistic scenario under the mild conditions of stationarity and mixing. In the special case where the errors are \textit{iid}, the standard error of $\phiad^*$ simplifies to the usual formula:
\begin{align}
    \text{se}_{\text{Naive}}(\hatphiad) = \sqrt{\sigmaad^2 \qad^{-1}},
\end{align}
\noindent where $\sigmaad^2$ is the autocorrelation-domain error variance.

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation/Time-Domain Standard Error}\label{sec:stderr-time-autocorrelation-domain}

As discussed, $\phiad^*$ defined in the autocorrelation domain by nonlinear projection \eqref{eq:nlm_loss} captures longer range autocorrelations than when it is defined in the time domain \eqref{eq:ar1-phi}. However, it assumes a signal + noise form for the ACF \eqref{eq:nlm} which is not realistic for an autoregressive process. For example, a correctly specified AR1 process has an ACF with no additive error ($\rho_k=\phiad^k$). For approximating higher order autoregressive processes, the residuals $e_k^*$ represent misspecification error -- the part of the ACF that the approximating model fails to explain. In many cases this error might be systematic and not random, and therefore the definition of standard error from \eqref{eq:stderr-autocorrelation-domain} would be incorrect. To address this problem, we propose a hybrid approach where the timescale is defined in the autocorrelation domain by \eqref{eq:nlm_loss} but its standard error is defined in the time domain by \eqref{eq:se-ar1-phi}.

\subsection{Standard Error Estimation}
\subsubsection{Time-Domain Standard Error Estimator}\label{sec:stderr-time-domain_}
The sample standard error estimator takes the form:
\begin{align}\label{eq:stderr-time-domain_}
\widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphitd) = \sqrt{\hatqtd^{-1}\;\hatomegatd\; \hatqtd^{-1}}, \quad
\widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hattautd) \approx \widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphitd) \cdot \frac{d}{d\phitd} g(\phitd)\big|_{\phitd=\hatphitd}
\end{align}

\noindent where
\begin{align}\label{eq:lls_q_omega_}
    \hatqtd = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=2}^T x_{t-1}^2 \quad\text{and}\quad
    \hatomegatd = \sum_{\ell=-M}^M \left(1 - \frac{|\ell|}{M+1}\right) \quad \frac{1}{T} \sum_{1\le t - \ell \le T} (x_{t-1} \cdot \hat e_t^*)(x_{t-1-\ell} \cdot \hat e_{t-\ell}^*).
\end{align}

\noindent This estimator calculates a weighted sum of the regression scores $x_{t-1} \cdot \hat e_t^*$, where $\hat e_t^* = x_t - \hatphitd \cdot x_{t-1}$. The true $\omegatd$ is approximated by $\hatomegatd$ by taking a finite sum of the regression score covariances up to lag $M$, where $M$ is the lag-truncation (or bandwidth). The weights used in the sum decrease linearly with lag $\ell$, following a Bartlett kernel \citep{newey_simple_1987}. This kernel not only ensures the standard errors remain non-negative but also regularizes $\hatomegatd$ to change smoothly with $M$ \citep[Chapter~14.35]{hansen_econometrics_2022}.\\

\noindent For comparison we also include the naive estimator which simplifies under correct specification:
\begin{align}
    \widehat{\text{se}}_\text{Naive}(\hatphitd) &= \sqrt{\hatsigmatd^2 \hatqtd^{-1}},\\
    \widehat{\text{se}}_\text{Naive}(\hattautd) &\approx \widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{Naive}}(\hatphitd) \frac{d}{d\phitd} g(\phitd)\big|_{\phitd=\hatphitd},
\end{align}

\noindent where $\hatsigmatd^2 = 1/T \sum_{t=2}^T \hat e_t^{*2}$ is an estimate of the time-domain error variance.

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation-Domain Standard Error Estimator}\label{sec:stderr-autocorrelation-domain_}
The sample standard error estimator takes the form:
\begin{align}\label{eq:stderr-autocorrelation-domain_}
\widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphiad) = \sqrt{\hatqad^{-1}\;\hatomegaad\; \hatqad^{-1}}, \quad
\widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hattauad) \approx \widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphiad) \cdot \frac{d}{d\phi} g(\phiad)\big|_{\phiad=\hatphiad},
\end{align}

\noindent where
\begin{align}
    \hatqad &= \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=0}^K (\hat m_{{\phiad},k})^2 = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=0}^K (k {\hatphiad}^{k-1})^2,\\
    \hatomegaad &= \sum_{\ell=-M}^M \left(1 - \frac{|\ell|}{M+1}\right) \quad \frac{1}{K} \sum_{1 \le k - \ell \le K} (\hat m_{{\phiad}, k} \cdot \hat e_k^*) (\hat m_{{\phiad}, k-\ell} \cdot \hat e_{k-\ell}^*).\label{eq:nls_q_omega_}
\end{align}

\noindent This estimator calculates a weighted sum of the linearized regression scores $\hat m_{\phiad, k} \cdot \hat e_k^*$, where $\hat e_k^* = \hat\rho_k - (\hatphiad)^k$. The estimator $\hatomegaad$ takes a finite sum of these scores up to lag $M$, weighted by a Bartlett kernel, so that $\hatomegaad$ changes smoothly with $M$.\\

\noindent In the case of correct specification the equation simplifies to:
\begin{align}
    \widehat{\text{se}}_\text{Naive}(\hatphiad) &= \sqrt{\hatsigmaad^2 \hatqad^{-1}},\\
    \widehat{\text{se}}_\text{Naive}(\hattauad &\approx \widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{Naive}}(\hatphiad) \frac{d}{d\phiad} g(\phiad)\big|_{\phiad=\hatphiad},
\end{align}

\noindent where $\hatsigmaad^2 = 1/K \sum_{k=0}^K \hat e_k^{*2}$ is an estimate of the autocorrelation-domain error variance.\\

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation/Time-Domain Standard Error Estimator}\label{sec:stderr-autocorrelation/time-domain_}
The sample standard error for the hybrid method is equivalent to \eqref{eq:stderr-time-domain_}, except that the time-domain estimator $\hatphitd$ is replaced with the autocorrelation-domain estimator $\hatphiad$, which redefines the errors to be in the time domain $\hat e_t^* = x_t - \hatphiad \cdot x_{t-1}$.

\subsection{Estimator Properties}\label{sec:estimator-properties}
In this section, we describe the large-sample properties of both the \nameref{sec:time-domain-linear-model} and \nameref{sec:autocorrelation-domain-nonlinear-model}, focusing on the consistency and limiting variance of their respective estimators. Under general conditions (see \nameref{sec:assumptions}) we demonstrate that the asymptotic distribution is Gaussian, with a limiting variance that can be consistently estimated. Consequently, the resulting t-ratios (see equation~\eqref{eq:t-ratio}) are also asymptotically Gaussian. This allows for the construction of hypothesis tests and confidence intervals across timescale maps of the brain.

\subsubsection{Time-Domain Method}
Following the description in \citet[Theorem~14.29]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, the ergodic theorem shows that mixing (which implies ergodicity) is a sufficient condition for \textit{consistent estimation}. Since $X_t$ is stationary and ergodic, so too are $X_t X_{t-1}$ and $X_{t-1}^2$, and as $T \to \infty$:
\begin{align}
    \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=2}^T x_t x_{t-1} &\underset{p}{\to} \mathbb{E}[X_t X_{t-1}],\\
    \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=2}^T x_{t-1}^2 &\underset{p}{\to} \mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2].
\end{align}

\noindent Applying the continuous mapping theorem yields:
\begin{align}
    \hat\phi^*_{\scriptscriptstyle\text{LLS}} = \left(\frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=2}^T x_{t-1}^2\right)^{-1} \left( \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=2}^T x_t x_{t-1}\right) &\underset{p}{\to} \mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2]^{-1}\mathbb{E}[X_t X_{t-1}] = \phi^*.
\end{align}

\noindent This shows that the coefficients of the \nameref{sec:time-domain-linear-model} can be consistently estimated by least squares, for any stationary and mixing process with parameters defined by projection in equation~\eqref{eq:ar1-phi}. Similarly for the regression score estimator in equation~\eqref{eq:lls_q_omega_}:
\begin{align}
    \hat \omega \underset{p}{\to} \omega.
\end{align}

Following \citet[Theorem~14.33]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, the asymptotic distribution under general dependence states that the \textit{limiting variance} of $\phi$ can be approximated using a central limit theorem for correlated observations. With the sample standard errors define in equation~\eqref{eq:stderr-time-domain_}, as $T\to\infty$:
\begin{align} \label{eq:ar1-phi-clt}
\frac{\hat\phi^*_{\scriptscriptstyle\text{LLS}} - \phi^*}{\widehat{\text{se}}_{NW}(\hat\phi^*_{\scriptscriptstyle\text{LLS}})} \underset{d}{\to} \mathcal{N}(0, 1).
\end{align}

\noindent And by the delta method we obtain the limiting variance for the timescale $\tau$, for the denominator defined in \eqref{eq:stderr-time-domain_}:
\begin{align}
    \frac{\hat{\tau}^*_{\scriptscriptstyle\text{LLS}} - \tau^*}{\widehat{\text{se}}_{NW}(\hat{\tau}^*_{\scriptscriptstyle\text{LLS}}) } \underset{d}{\to} \mathcal{N}(0,1).
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation-Domain Method}

To show \textit{consistent estimation}, unlike the time-domain method above where we apply the ergodic theorem to the explicit closed-form expression of the estimator, this is not possible for nonlinear estimators because there is no algebraic expression. Instead, nonlinear least squares minimizes the sample objective function $\widehat{S}(\phi)$ from equation~\eqref{eq:nls_loss_}, which is itself a sample average. By \citet[Theorem~22.1]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, for any $\phi$, the weak law of large numbers shows that:
\begin{align}
\widehat{S}(\phi) &\underset{p}{\to} S(\phi).
\end{align}

\noindent Further, if the minimizer $\phi^*$ is unique, $S(\phi) > S(\phi^*)$ for all $\phi \ne \phi^*$, then the sample minimizer from equation~\eqref{eq:nls_phi_} converges in probability to the true minimum as $K\to\infty$:
\begin{align}
\hat \phi^*_{\scriptscriptstyle\text{NLS}} &\underset{p}{\to} \phi^*.
\end{align}

\noindent This shows that the parameters of the \nameref{sec:autocorrelation-domain-nonlinear-model} can be consistently estimated by least squares. Similarly for the regression score estimator in equation~\eqref{eq:nls_q_omega_}:
\begin{align}
\hat \omega \underset{p}{\to} \omega.
\end{align}


With the additional assumption that the objective function \eqref{eq:nlm_loss} is Lipschitz-continuous for $\phi$ near $\phi^*$, following \citet[Theorem~23.2]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, we can approximate the \textit{limiting variance} of $\phi^*$ and $\tau^*$ using a central limit theorem for correlated observations. Under general conditions, the nonlinear least squares estimator has an asymptotic distribution with a similar structure to that of the linear least squares estimator above; it converges to a Gaussian distribution with a sandwhich-form variance. With the sample standard errors define in \eqref{eq:stderr-autocorrelation-domain_}, as $K\to\infty$:
\begin{align}
\frac{\hat\phi^*_{\scriptscriptstyle\scriptscriptstyle\text{NLS}} - \phi^*}{\widehat{\text{se}}_{NW}(\hat\phi^*_{\scriptscriptstyle\text{NLS}})} \underset{d}{\to} \mathcal{N}(0, 1). \label{eq:nls-phi-clt}
\end{align}

\noindent And by the delta method we obtain the limiting variance for the timescale $\tau^*$, for the denominator defined in \eqref{eq:stderr-autocorrelation-domain_}:
\begin{align}
\frac{\hat{\tau^*} - \tau^*}{\widehat{\text{se}}_{NW}(\hat{\tau^*}_{\scriptscriptstyle\text{NLS}})} \underset{d}{\to} \mathcal{N}(0,1).
\end{align}
