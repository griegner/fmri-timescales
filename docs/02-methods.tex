\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

There are varying definitions and conventions in the study of time series data and stochastic processes more generally. To simplify our presentation, this work closely follows the \citet{hansen_econometrics_2022} \emph{Econometrics} textbook as a primary reference. Throughout, we will frequently refer the reader to specific results from this textbook.

\subsection{Assumptions} \label{sec:assumptions}

Let $\{X_t\}_{t\in\{\dots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \dots\}}$ be a discrete-time stochastic process that is \nameref{sec:weak-stationarity} and \nameref{sec:strong-mixing} (abbreviated as ``stationary'' and ``mixing'' throughout), and let $x = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T\}$ where $x_t$ denotes an observed sample of $X_t$. For simplicity, assume $\mathbb{E}[X_t] = 0$. 

\subsubsection{Weakly stationary} \label{sec:weak-stationarity}
Weak stationarity implies a constant mean and variance (independent of time index $t$), and an autocovariance function that only depends on time lag $k$, $\gamma_k = \text{cov}[X_t, X_{t-k}] =\mathbb{E}[X_t X_{t-k}]$. For analysis, we use a normalized measure of the autocovariances, the \textit{autocorrelation function (ACF)}:
\begin{align} \label{eq:acf}
\rho_k = \text{corr}(X_t, X_{t-k}) = \gamma_k/\gamma_0,
\end{align}

\noindent where $\gamma_k$ is the autocovariance at lag $k$ and $\gamma_0$ is the variance. We emphasize that $\rho_k$ represents \textit{temporal} autocorrelation, not spatial autocorrelation often referenced in the fMRI literature. As a correlation, $|\rho_k|\le 1$, but stationarity alone does not guarantee decay of $\rho_k$ with increasing time lag; constant or periodic processes can maintain nonzero correlations indefinitely. 

\subsubsection{Strong Mixing} \label{sec:strong-mixing}
Strong mixing ($\alpha$-mixing) imposes stronger dependence constraints than stationarity while still allowing for a wide set of stochastic processes. By definition, a process is strong mixing if $\alpha(\ell) \rightarrow 0$ as $\ell \rightarrow \infty$, where $\alpha(\ell)$ measures the \emph{maximum dependence} between events separated by $\ell$ time points; this controls the full joint dependence structure and implies distant events are uncorrelated. Strong mixing also implies ergodicity \citep[Chapter~14.12]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, which ensures consistent estimation by the ergodic theorem \citep[Theorem~14.9]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. Additionally, if a mixing process has $r>2$ finite moments $\mathbb{E}|X_t|^r < \infty$ and its mixing coefficients satisfy $\sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty} \alpha(\ell)^{1-2/r} < \infty$, then its autocorrelations decay sufficiently fast for application of the central limit theorem for dependent data \citep[Theorem~14.15]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. These conditions enable asymptotic theory for estimation and inference on timescale maps (see \nameref{sec:estimator-properties}).\\

As introduced by \citet{murray_hierarchy_2014}, the timescale $\tau$ represents the lag where exponentially decaying autocorrelations reach $1/e \approx 0.37$, analogous to the time constants of many physical systems and often referred to as $e$-folding time in other fields. While it provides an intuitive description of the memory or persistence of that process, assuming an exponential function imposes stricter constraints than strong mixing, which alone does not prescribe any specific type of decay (exponential, linear, damped periodic, etc.). Despite this, the timescale serves as a useful summary statistic for the duration of temporal dependence, enabling direct comparisons across brain regions even when the true decay structure is more complex. This highlights an important distinction between the data-generating process and the simplified parametric model used to describe the timescale at which such a process becomes decorrelated. In the present paper, we adopt broad assumptions, requiring only that the process is stationary and mixing, to account for cases where the ACF decay may not be strictly exponential.

\subsubsection{Estimation and Inference under Misspecification}\label{sec:misspecification}

We approximate the dominant exponential decay in autocorrelations by a single timescale parameter $\tau$, and formally evaluate two timescale methods that are commonly applied across neuroimaging modalities (fMRI, EEG, ECoG, MEG). The time-domain (TD) linear model estimated with linear least squares \citep{kaneoke_variance_2012, meisel_decline_2017, huang_timescales_2018, lurie_cortical_2024, shinn_functional_2023, shafiei_topographic_2020, spitmaan_multiple_2020, trepka_training-dependent_2024}, and the autocorrelation-domain (AD) nonlinear model estimated with nonlinear least squares \citep{rossi-pool_invariant_2021, cirillo_neural_2018, ito_cortical_2020, runyan_distinct_2017, zeraati_flexible_2022, nougaret_intrinsic_2021, wasmuht_intrinsic_2018, muller_core_2020, maisson_choice-relevant_2021, li_hierarchical_2022, shafiei_topographic_2020}. Both belong to the subclass of M-estimators defined by minimizing a sample squared error loss function. This approach seeks the best approximation in terms of squared error and does not require full specification of the data-generating distribution, relying on method of moments instead of maximum likelihood estimation. \\

Throughout, a superscript star (e.g. $\tau^*$) denotes the pseudo-true parameter corresponding to the best fitting parameter in a possibly misspecified model. If correctly specified, $\tau^*$ is the true timescale; otherwise, it is an approximation. Acknowledging that the data-generating process and fitted model will likely be different in practice, we introduce standard errors that account for this mismatch, enabling valid inference despite misspecification of the autocorrelation decay. Under the above assumptions and relevant moment conditions, the M-estimators are consistent for the pseudo-true parameter and asymptotically normal (see \nameref{sec:estimator-properties}). Further, they are invariant under the reparameterization needed to define timescales, with asymptotic normality preserved by the delta method, ensuring valid inference for transformed parameters.

\subsection{Timescale Definitions}

\subsubsection{Time-Domain Linear Model}\label{sec:time-domain-linear-model}
A first order autoregressive model (AR1) provides a linear approximation of the timescale. The AR1 model:
\begin{align}\label{eq:ar1}
    X_t = \phitd X_{t-1} + e_t,
\end{align}

\noindent defines the process as a linear regression between $X_t$ and $X_{t-1}$ in the time domain with \textit{iid} innovations $e_t$ with variance $\sigmatd^2 = \mathbb{E}[e_t^2]$. In the autocorrelation domain, it implies that the theoretical ACF decays exponentially at a rate determined by $\phitd$, such that $\rho_k = \phitd^k$ \citep[Chapter~14.22]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. For a stationary process with $|\phitd|<1$, the exponential decay rate can be directly obtained from $\phitd$, with a timescale $\tautd$ equal to the lag at which the AR1-projected ACF reaches $1/e\approx 0.37$, that is,  $\rho_\tau = \phitd^\tau = 1/e$, resulting in $\tautd = g(\phitd) = -1/\text{log}(|\phitd|)$. The timescale $\tautd$ is expressed as a nonlinear function of $\phitd$, denoted by $g(\phitd)$. This defines $\tautd$ to be a real number even though the ACF only includes integer indices. The absolute value allows for $\phitd<0$ and is introduced as a modeling assumption to ensure the timescale captures the monotonic decay rate of autocorrelations, consistent with the approach of \citet{murray_hierarchy_2014}.\\

The parameter $\phitd^*$ is the value that minimizes the expected squared error function $S(\phitd)$:
\begin{align}
    S(\phitd) = \mathbb{E}[(X_t - \phitd X_{t-1})^2],\quad \phitd^* = \underset{\phitd}{\text{argmin}} \; S(\phitd).
\end{align}

\noindent $S(\phitd)$ is minimized by taking its derivative with respect to $\phitd$, setting it to zero, and solving for $\phitd^*$:
\begin{align}
    \frac{d}{d\phitd} S(\phitd) = -2 \mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}(X_t - \phitd X_{t-1})] = 0.
\end{align}

\noindent Differentiating the quadratic function yields a linear equation in $\phitd$, and solving this results in a closed-form expression for the optimal $\phitd^*$. Therefore, $\phitd^*$ is defined by \textit{linear projection} and the timescale parameter $\tautd^*$ by a change of variable:
\begin{align}
    \phitd^* &= (\mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2])^{-1}(\mathbb{E}[X_t X_{t-1}]) \label{eq:ar1-phi},\\
    \tautd^* &= g(\phitd^*) = -\frac{1}{\text{log}(|\phitd^*|)}. \label{eq:ar1-tau}
\end{align}

\noindent Thus, the timescale represents the characteristic time of $1/e$ autocorrelation decay derived from the best-fitting AR1 model under squared error loss. Since $X_t$ is stationary with finite variance, the parameters $\phitd^*$ and $\tautd^*$ defined by projection are unique; in fact, any approximating AR1 model is identifiable if $\mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2]$ is nonzero and finite \citep[Theorem~14.28]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. Importantly, the observed process $X_t$ need not follow the AR1 model from equation~\eqref{eq:ar1} with \textit{iid} innovations $e_t$. This allows for projection errors, i.e., residuals from linear projection onto the working model:
\begin{align}
    e_t^* = X_t - \phitd^* X_{t-1},
\end{align}

\noindent which may exhibit unequal variance and autocorrelation. This applies to any stationary and mixing process, even if the true data-generating process is not AR1, so the resulting fit is an AR1 projection.

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation-Domain Nonlinear Model}\label{sec:autocorrelation-domain-nonlinear-model}

Alternatively, timescales can be defined in the autocorrelation domain by an exponential decay function, as introduced by \citet{murray_hierarchy_2014}. We formalize this by projecting the $K$-lagged sequence of autocorrelations $(\rho_1, \dots, \rho_K)$ from \eqref{eq:acf} onto an exponential approximation $\rho_k \approx \phiad^k$ with decay parameter $\phiad$, minimizing the sum of squared errors averaged over fixed $K$:
\begin{align}
    S_K(\phiad) &= \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \left( \rho_k - \phiad^k \right)^2, \label{eq:nlm_loss} \\
    \phiad^* &= \underset{\phiad}{\text{argmin}} \; S_K(\phiad). \label{eq:nlm_phi}
\end{align}

Unlike the \nameref{sec:time-domain-linear-model}, which effectively projects using only a single lag, this definition captures exponential decay across a sequence of multiple ($K$) lags, incorporating longer-range temporal dependencies. Consequently, the pseudo-true parameter $\phiad^*$ is distinct from the time-domain parameter $\phitd^*$, as its value and interpretation differ when aggregated over multiple lags. Crucially, the projection residuals $\rho_k - \phiad^{*k}$ are deterministic population quantities defined purely as a consequence of projection, not random variables. These residuals represent the systematic misspecification of projecting the true autocorrelation function -- which could be multi-exponential or damped periodic under general stationary and mixing conditions -- onto the closest single-parameter exponential decay. In the case of correct specification these residuals are exactly zero.

$S_K(\phiad)$ is minimized by taking its derivative with respect to $\phiad$, setting it to zero, and solving for $\phiad$:
\begin{align}
    \frac{d}{d\phiad} S_K(\phiad) &= -\frac{2}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K k\phiad^{k-1}(\rho_k - \phiad^k) = 0.
\end{align}

\noindent However, this derivative is nonlinear in $\phiad$, preventing a closed-form solution. The first-order condition sums nonlinear terms across many lags, yielding a high-order equation with no general algebraic solution. Therefore, the pseudo-true parameter $\phiad^*$ is defined implicitly as the solution to this nonlinear equation and must be computed via numerical optimization. As in the time-domain case, the corresponding timescale is defined as the time lag at which the fitted ACF reaches $1/e$ and can be expressed by the change of variable:
\begin{align}
    \tauad^* &= g(\phiad^*) = -\frac{1}{\text{log}(|\phiad^*|)}. \label{eq:nlm-tau}
\end{align}

\subsection{Timescale Estimation}\label{sec:timescale-estimation}
\subsubsection{Time-Domain Linear Estimator}\label{sec:time-domain-linear-estimator}
Given observations $x_1, \ldots, x_T$ are demeaned, the linear least squares estimator of the \nameref{sec:time-domain-linear-model} is obtained by replacing the expectations in equation~\eqref{eq:ar1-phi} using method of moments. It has the following closed-form expression:
\begin{align}
    \hatphitd &= \left(\sum_{t=2}^{T+1} x_{t-1}^2\right)^{-1} \left(\sum_{t=2}^T x_t x_{t-1}\right),\label{eq:ar1-phi_}\\
    \hattautd &= g(\hatphitd) = -\frac{1}{\text{log}(|\hatphitd|)},\label{eq:ar1-tau_}
\end{align}

\noindent where \eqref{eq:ar1-phi_} and \eqref{eq:ar1-tau_} are the sample versions of the population parameters from equations~\eqref{eq:ar1-phi} and \eqref{eq:ar1-tau}, respectively \citep[Chapter~14.3]{hansen_econometrics_2022}.\\


\subsubsection{Autocorrelation-Domain Nonlinear Estimator}\label{sec:autocorrelation-domain-nonlinear-estimator}
The nonlinear least squares estimator of the \nameref{sec:autocorrelation-domain-nonlinear-model} is fit to the ACF, so the time series needs to be first transformed into the autocorrelation domain. For a finite-length and centered time series, the population ACF from equation~\eqref{eq:acf} is estimated by:
\begin{align}\label{eq:acf_}
    \hat\rho_k &= \hat\gamma_0^{-1}\hat\gamma_k = \left(\sum_{t=1}^T x_t^2\right)^{-1} \left(\sum_{t=k+1}^{T}x_t x_{t-k}\right),
\end{align}

\noindent where $\hat\gamma_k$ is the sample covariance at lag $k$ and $\hat\gamma_0$ is the sample variance. Under the mixing assumption, the population ACF \eqref{eq:acf} approaches zero as lag $k$ increases. However, sampling variability may yield non-zero autocorrelations even when true values are zero. To mitigate this, the sample ACF estimator \eqref{eq:acf_} imposes a bias towards zero by scaling the autocovariances ($\hat \gamma_k$, calculated using $T-k$ terms) by the total sample variance ($\hat\gamma_0$, calculated using all $T$ timepoints). The finite sample performance of this ACF estimator was compared to other standard estimators and shown to perform best in terms of bias and variance \citep{marriott_bias_1954}.\\

The exponential decay parameter $\phiad^*$ that minimizes the cost function $S_K(\phiad)$ in equation~\eqref{eq:nlm_loss} is estimated by minimizing the sample analog $\widehat{S}_{K}(\phiad)$:
\begin{align}
    \widehat{S}_{K}(\phiad) &= \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K (\hat\rho_k - \phiad^k)^2, \label{eq:nls_loss_}\label{eq:nlm_loss_}\\
    \hatphiad &= \underset{\phiad}{\text{argmin}} \; \widehat{S}_{K}(\phiad), \label{eq:nlm_phi_}\\
    \hattauad &= g(\hatphiad) = -\frac{1}{\text{log}(|\hatphiad|)}.\label{eq:nlm_tau_}
\end{align}

\noindent Here, \eqref{eq:nlm_phi_} and \eqref{eq:nlm_tau_} are the sample versions of the population parameters from equations~\eqref{eq:nlm_phi} and \eqref{eq:nlm-tau}, respectively. In this paper we use the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm to iteratively update the estimate of $\hatphiad$ until convergence (i.e., when the step size goes below a $10^{-6}$ tolerance) \citep{levenberg_method_1944}.

\subsection{Estimator Properties}\label{sec:estimator-properties}
In this section, we describe the large-sample properties of both the \nameref{sec:time-domain-linear-model} and \nameref{sec:autocorrelation-domain-nonlinear-model}, focusing on the consistency and asymptotic distribution of their respective estimators. Under general conditions --- when the time-domain method is applied to a process that is not AR1, or the autocorrelation-domain method is applied to a decay process that is not exponential --- we demonstrate that the asymptotic distribution is Gaussian, with a limiting variance that can be consistently estimated. Specific expressions for this variance and its estimator are provided after in sections \nameref{sec:stderr} and \nameref{sec:stderr_}. This allows for the construction of hypothesis tests and confidence intervals across timescale maps of the brain.

\subsubsection{Time-Domain Method}
Following the description in \citet[Theorem~14.29]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, the ergodic theorem shows that mixing (which implies ergodicity) is a sufficient condition for \textit{consistent estimation}. Since $X_t$ is stationary and ergodic, so too are $X_t X_{t-1}$ and $X_{t-1}^2$, and as $T \to \infty$:
\begin{align}
    \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=2}^T x_t x_{t-1} &\overset{p}{\to} \mathbb{E}[X_t X_{t-1}],\\
    \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=2}^T x_{t-1}^2 &\overset{p}{\to} \mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2].
\end{align}

\noindent Applying the continuous mapping theorem yields:
\begin{align}
    \hatphitd = \left(\frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=2}^T x_{t-1}^2\right)^{-1} \left( \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=2}^T x_t x_{t-1}\right) &\overset{p}{\to} \mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2]^{-1}\mathbb{E}[X_t X_{t-1}] = \phitd^*.
\end{align}

\noindent This shows that the coefficients of the \nameref{sec:time-domain-linear-model} can be consistently estimated by least squares, for any stationary and mixing process with parameters defined by projection in equation~\eqref{eq:ar1-phi}.\\

Following \citet[Theorem~14.33]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, the asymptotic distribution under general dependence states that the behavior of the estimators of $\phitd^*$ and $\tautd^*$ can be approximated using a central limit theorem for correlated observations:

\begin{align}\label{eq:ar1-phi-clt}
    \sqrt{T}(\hatphitd - \phitd^*) \overset{d}{\to} \mathcal{N}(0, \qtd^{-1}\; \omegatd \; \qtd^{-1})\;\text{as}\;T\to\infty.
\end{align}

\noindent The estimator converges to a Gaussian distribution with a sandwich-form variance with components $\qtd$ and $\omegatd$ defined below in equation \eqref{eq:se-ar1-qomega}. And by the delta method we obtain the asymptotic distribution for the timescale given the transformation from equation \eqref{eq:ar1-tau}:

\begin{align}
    \sqrt{T}(\hattautd - \tautd^*) \overset{d}{\to} \mathcal{N}\left(0, \qtd^{-1}\; \omegatd \; \qtd^{-1} \left[\frac{d}{d\phitd} g(\phitd)\bigg|_{\phitd=\phitd^*}\right]^2\right)\;\text{as}\;T\to\infty.
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation-Domain Method}

To show \textit{consistent estimation}, the sample autocorrelation $\hat\rho_k$ \eqref{eq:acf_} is a ratio of sample averages in the time domain, and under the ergodic and continuous mapping theorems \citep[Theorem~14.9]{hansen_econometrics_2022}:
\begin{align}
    \hat\rho_k \overset{p}{\to} \rho_k \;\text{as}\;T\to\infty\;\text{for all }k\in{1, \dots, K}.
\end{align}

\noindent Consequently, the sample objective function \eqref{eq:nlm_loss_} is itself a sample average, such that for any fixed $K$ and $\phiad$:
\begin{align}
    \hat{S}_K(\phiad) = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K (\hat\rho_k - \phiad^k)^2 \overset{p}{\to} \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K (\rho_k - \phiad^k)^2 = S_K(\phiad).
\end{align}

\noindent Further, if the minimizer $\phiad^*$ is unique, $S(\phiad)>S(\phiad^*)$ for all $\phiad\ne\phiad^*$, then the sample minimizer from \eqref{eq:nlm_phi_} converges to the true minimum:
\begin{align}
    \hatphiad \overset{p}{\to} \phiad^* \;\text{as}\;T\to\infty.
\end{align}

\noindent This shows that the parameters of the \nameref{sec:autocorrelation-domain-nonlinear-model} can be consistently estimated by least squares. For this theoretical convergence to hold in practice, we assume that the numerical tolerance of the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm approaches zero as $T \to \infty$.\\

To derive the asymptotic distribution, we utilize a first-order dominance approximation. The estimator $\hatphiad$ minimizes the squared distance between sample and model autocorrelations. The first-order condition for minimization is:
\begin{align}
\sum_{k=1}^K k \phi^{k-1} (\hat{\rho}_k - \phi^k) = 0.
\end{align}

\noindent For decaying processes where $|\phi| < 1$, the gradient weights $k \phi^{k-1}$ decrease rapidly with lag $k$. Consequently, the determination of $\hatphiad$ is overwhelmingly dominated by the first lag ($k=1$), where the weight is unity, compared to higher lags where weights approach zero. Therefore, the sampling variability of $\hatphiad$ is well-approximated by the sampling variability of the first autocorrelation $\hat{\rho}_1$.\\

Formally, the exact variance of $\hatphiad$ depends on the joint asymptotic covariance matrix of the full sequence of autocorrelations $(\hat{\rho}_1, \dots, \hat{\rho}_K)$. By focusing on the dominance of the first lag, this approximation essentially sets the weights of higher-order lags ($k > 1$) to zero, thereby ignoring the contributions of $\text{Var}(\hat{\rho}_k)$ for $k>1$ and the cross-lag covariances $\text{Cov}(\hat{\rho}_1, \hat{\rho}_k)$. While this omits secondary information, it captures the primary source of variance for rapidly decaying processes.\\

We can thus approximate the asymptotic distribution of $\hatphiad$ using the Central Limit Theorem for the first lag, as established for the time-domain estimator \citep[Theorem~14.33]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. This yields a Gaussian approximation with the familiar sandwich variance:
\begin{align}
\sqrt{T}(\hatphiad - \phiad^*) \overset{d}{\approx} \mathcal{N}(0, \qad^{-1}; \omegaad ;\qad^{-1}) ;\text{as};T\to\infty.
\end{align}

\noindent Under this approximation, the variance components $\qad$ and $\omegaad$ are defined identically to the time-domain components in equation (...), but evaluated using the autocorrelation-domain parameter $\phiad^*$ instead of the projection parameter $\phitd^*$:
\begin{align}\label{eq:ad-qomega}
\qad = \mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2] \quad\text{and}\quad
\omegaad = \sum_{\ell=-\infty}^{\infty} \mathbb{E}[(X_{t-1} \cdot e_t^)(X_{t-1-\ell} \cdot e_{t-\ell}^)],
\end{align}

\noindent where the residuals are defined as $e_t^* = X_t - \phiad^* X_{t-1}$. This formulation captures the time-domain dependence (HAC) and heteroskedasticity inherent in the first lag, which is the primary driver of the estimator's variance.\\

...

We approximate the asymptotic distribution by taking a linear Taylor expansion of the first-order condition around the true parameter $\phiad^$. This yields a Gaussian distribution with a sandwich variance that incorporates the joint asymptotic covariance of the full lag sequence $(\hat{\rho}_1, \dots, \hat{\rho}_K)$:
\begin{align}
\sqrt{T}(\hatphiad - \phiad^) \overset{d}{\to} \mathcal{N}(0, \qad^{-1}; \omegaad ;\qad^{-1}) ;\text{as};T\to\infty.
\end{align}

\noindent Here, $\qad$ represents the Hessian of the objective function (the squared gradient weights), and $\omegaad$ represents the long-run variance of the composite score $Z_t$. The composite score is the weighted sum of the time-domain scores for each lag $k$:
\begin{align}\label{eq:ad-qomega}
\qad &= \sum_{k=1}^K (k \phiad^{*k-1})^2, \
\omegaad &= \sum_{\ell=-\infty}^{\infty} \mathbb{E}[Z_t \cdot Z_{t-\ell}], \quad \text{where } Z_t = \sum_{k=1}^K \left( k \phiad^{*k-1} \right) \left[ X_{t-k}(X_t - \phiad^{*k} X_{t-k}) \right].
\end{align}

\noindent In this formulation, $Z_t$ aggregates the residuals from all $K$ lags, weighted by their sensitivity $k\phiad^{*k-1}$. The summation in $\omegaad$ (HAC estimator) thus captures not only the serial correlation within each lag but also the cross-correlations between different lags, providing a fully robust standard error for the multi-lag estimator.\\

...

To show \textit{consistent estimation}, the sample autocorrelation $\hat\rho_k$ \eqref{eq:acf_} is a ratio of sample averages in the time domain. Under the ergodic and continuous mapping theorems \citep[Theorem~14.9]{hansen_econometrics_2022}:
\begin{align}
\hat\rho_k \overset{p}{\to} \rho_k ;\text{as};T\to\infty;\text{for all }k\in{1, \dots, K}.
\end{align}

\noindent Consequently, the sample objective function \eqref{eq:nlm_loss_} converges pointwise to the population objective function, and the estimator $\hatphiad$ converges to the pseudo-true parameter $\phiad^*$ \citep[Theorem 22.1]{hansen_econometrics_2022}.

To derive the asymptotic distribution, we define the score function of the estimator. The estimator $\hatphiad$ is the solution to the first-order condition $\psi_T(\hatphiad) = 0$, where:
\begin{align}
\psi_T(\phi) = \frac{d}{d\phi} \hat{S}K(\phi) = -2 \sum{k=1}^K k \phi^{k-1} (\hat{\rho}_k - \phi^k).
\end{align}

\noindent Following the standard proof for asymptotic normality of M-estimators \citep[Theorem 22.3 and Section 23.4]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, we take a mean-value expansion (linear Taylor expansion) of the score function $\psi_T(\hatphiad)$ around the true parameter $\phiad^*$:
\begin{align}
0 = \psi_T(\hatphiad) \approx \psi_T(\phiad^) + \frac{d}{d\phi}\psi_T(\phiad^) (\hatphiad - \phiad^).
\end{align}

\noindent Rearranging terms and scaling by $\sqrt{T}$ yields:
\begin{align}
\sqrt{T}(\hatphiad - \phiad^) \approx -\left( \frac{d}{d\phi}\psi_T(\phiad^) \right)^{-1} \sqrt{T} \psi_T(\phiad^*).
\end{align}

\noindent The first term (the Hessian) converges in probability to the curvature constant $Q$ by the Weak Law of Large Numbers. The second term (the normalized score at the true parameter) converges in distribution to a Normal random variable by the Central Limit Theorem for mixing processes \citep[Theorem 14.33]{hansen_econometrics_2022}.

This yields a Gaussian distribution with a sandwich variance that incorporates the joint asymptotic covariance of the full lag sequence:
\begin{align}
\sqrt{T}(\hatphiad - \phiad^*) \overset{d}{\to} \mathcal{N}(0, \qad^{-1}; \omegaad ;\qad^{-1}) ;\text{as};T\to\infty.
\end{align}

\noindent Here, $\qad$ is the population limit of the Hessian (scaled by 2), representing the curvature of the objective function:
\begin{align}
\qad &= \sum_{k=1}^K (k \phiad^{*k-1})^2.
\end{align}

\noindent $\omegaad$ is the long-run variance of the composite score $Z_t$. By rewriting the first-order condition in terms of the underlying time series data $X_t$, we see that the total deviation is a weighted sum of the deviations at each lag:
\begin{align}
\omegaad &= \sum_{\ell=-\infty}^{\infty} \mathbb{E}[Z_t \cdot Z_{t-\ell}], \quad \text{where } Z_t = \sum_{k=1}^K \left( k \phiad^{*k-1} \right) \left[ X_{t-k}(X_t - \phiad^{*k} X_{t-k}) \right].
\end{align}

\noindent In this formulation, $Z_t$ is a scalar random variable that aggregates the residuals from all $K$ lags, weighted by their gradient sensitivity $k\phiad^{*k-1}$. The summation in $\omegaad$ (a standard HAC estimator applied to the scalar series $Z_t$) thus captures not only the serial correlation within each lag but also the cross-correlations between different lags, providing a fully robust standard error for the multi-lag estimator.

\subsection{Standard Error of the Estimators}\label{sec:stderr}
\subsubsection{Time-Domain Standard Error}\label{sec:stderr-time-domain}
Having established in \nameref{sec:estimator-properties} that the estimators are asymptotically normal, we now derive the specific variance components required for inference. When the data-generating process deviates from AR1 such that the errors are dependent and/or heteroskedastic, the usual (naive) standard errors will be biased. As a result, confidence intervals or hypothesis tests that rely on them are invalid. To correct for this, the \citet{newey_simple_1987} (NW) expression takes a sandwich form and explicitly accounts for misspecification by summing the covariance structure of the errors, ensuring that the resulting standard errors are asymptotically valid \citep[Theorem 14.32]{hansen_econometrics_2022}.\\

Given that $X_t$ is stationary and mixing, so too are the errors from equation~\eqref{eq:ar1} since these properties are preserved by finite transformations \citep[Theorem~14.2 and Theorem~14.12]{hansen_econometrics_2022}. Consequently, the autocovariances of the errors vanish as the time lag increases (see \nameref{sec:assumptions}). Further, because the timescale estimator $\hattautd$ is given by the nonlinear function $g(\hatphitd)$, its theoretical standard error can be approximated by the delta method, which uses the derivative with respect to $\phitd$ evaluated at the pseudo-true parameter value $\phitd^*$:
\begin{align}
    \text{se}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphitd) = \sqrt{\qtd^{-1}\; \omegatd \; \qtd^{-1}} \label{eq:se-ar1-phi}, \quad
    \text{se}_{\text{NW}}(\hattautd) \approx \text{se}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphitd) \cdot \frac{d}{d\phitd} g(\phitd)\Big|_{\phitd=\phitd^*},
\end{align}

\noindent where
\begin{align}\label{eq:se-ar1-qomega}
    \qtd = \mathbb{E}[X_{t-1}^2] \quad\text{and}\quad \omegatd = \sum_{\ell=-\infty}^{\infty} \mathbb{E}[(X_{t-1} \cdot e_t^*)(X_{t-1-\ell} \cdot e_{t-\ell}^*)].
\end{align}

\noindent These moments, $\qtd$ and $\omegatd$, are stationary and therefore do not depend on time index $t$. The covariance terms in $\omegatd$ capture deviations in the error structure from the standard \textit{iid} case. For the special case of correct specification, when $X_t$ is a true AR1 process, the standard error of $\hatphitd$ reduces to the usual formula:
\begin{align}
    \text{se}_{\text{Naive}}(\hatphitd) = \sqrt{\sigmatd^2 \qtd^{-1}},
\end{align}

\noindent where $\sigmatd^2$ is the time-domain error variance. \\

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation-Domain Standard Error}\label{sec:stderr-autocorrelation-domain}
Equation~\eqref{eq:nlm} models autocorrelation decay using a parametric exponential function. While usual (naive) applications assume \textit{iid} errors, our approach permits dependent errors generated by an underlying stationary and mixing process. The standard errors proposed below account for potential misspecification of exponential autocorrelation and are asymptotically valid under these more general conditions.\\

Following the description in \citet[Chapter~22.8 and Chapter~23.5]{hansen_econometrics_2022}, if $\phiad^*$ uniquely minimizes $S(\phiad)$ in equation~\eqref{eq:nlm_loss}, such that $S(\phiad) > S(\phiad^*)$ for all $\phiad \neq \phiad^*$, the theoretical standard error of the estimator $\hatphiad$ can be computed using a \citet{newey_simple_1987} (NW) form that reflects both the curvature of the squared loss function at its minimum and the covariance of the errors. Further, because the timescale estimator $\hattauad$ is given by the nonlinear function $g(\hatphiad)$, its theoretical standard error can be approximated by the delta method using the derivative with respect to $\phiad$ evaluated at the true parameter value $\phiad^*$:
\begin{align}\label{eq:stderr-autocorrelation-domain}
    \text{se}_\text{NW}(\hatphiad) = \sqrt{\qad^{-1}\; \omegaad \;\qad^{-1}}, \quad \text{se}_\text{NW}(\hattauad) \approx \text{se}_\text{NW}(\hatphiad) \cdot \frac{d}{d\phiad}g(\phiad)\Big|_{\phiad=\phiad^*}.
\end{align}

\noindent The components $\qad$ and $\omegaad$ are population quantities derived from the regression function $m(k, \phiad) = \phiad^k$ in \eqref{eq:nlm} and its derivative $m_{\phiad, k} = \frac{d}{d\phiad} m(k, \phiad) = k \phiad^{k-1}$, defined and evaluated at the minimizer $\phiad^*$ as:
\begin{align}\label{eq:ad-qomega}
    \qad = \mathbb{E}[m_{\phiad^*, k}^2] = \mathbb{E}[(k \phiad^{*k-1})^2] \quad\text{and}\quad
    \omegaad = \sum_{\ell=-\infty}^{\infty} \mathbb{E}[(m_{\phiad^*, k} \cdot e_{k})(m_{\phiad^*, k-\ell} \cdot e_{k-\ell})].
\end{align}

\noindent The derivative of the regression function $m_{\phiad^*, k}$ evaluated at $\phiad^*$ locally approximates the nonlinear model by a linear one, and the expression for $\omegaad$ sums the covariance structure of the errors, ensuring that the standard errors are asymptotically valid under model misspecification. This is a realistic scenario under the mild conditions of stationarity and mixing. In the special case where the errors are \textit{iid}, the standard error of $\phiad^*$ simplifies to the usual formula:
\begin{align}
    \text{se}_{\text{Naive}}(\hatphiad) = \sqrt{\sigmaad^2 \qad^{-1}},
\end{align}
\noindent where $\sigmaad^2$ is the autocorrelation-domain error variance.

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation/Time-Domain Standard Error}\label{sec:stderr-time-autocorrelation-domain}

As discussed, $\phiad^*$ defined in the autocorrelation domain captures longer-range autocorrelations than the time-domain parameter. However, the model \eqref{eq:nlm} assumes a signal-plus-noise structure that is unrealistic for an autoregressive process. For example, the ACF of a correctly specified AR1 process is deterministic ($\rho_k=\phiad^k$) with no additive error. In this case, the autocorrelation-domain residuals $e_k^*$ are zero, giving the false impression that the standard error vanishes. This is misleading because the parameter $\phiad^*$ is still a random variable with a sampling distribution governed by the stochastic time-domain innovations $e_t$; because this variability arises in the time domain, it must be estimated there.\\

For higher-order autoregressive processes, the residuals $e_k^*$ represent misspecification error -- the part of the ACF the model fails to explain. Unlike the irreducible and stochastic innovations, this error is reducible and systematic. Consequently, the standard error formula \eqref{eq:stderr-autocorrelation-domain}, which treats $e_k^*$ as random noise, is invalid. To address this, we propose a hybrid approach where the timescale is defined in the autocorrelation domain by \eqref{eq:nlm_loss} to capture long-range correlations, but its standard error is defined in the time domain by \eqref{eq:se-ar1-phi} to correctly capture the sampling variability arising from the innovations. This distinction becomes important in section \nameref{sec:simulations}, where we evaluate these methods on simulated autoregressive processes.

\subsection{Estimation of Standard Errors}\label{sec:stderr_}
\subsubsection{Time-Domain Standard Error Estimator}\label{sec:stderr-time-domain_}
The sample standard error estimator takes the form:
\begin{align}\label{eq:stderr-time-domain_}
\widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphitd) = \sqrt{\hatqtd^{-1}\;\hatomegatd\; \hatqtd^{-1}}, \quad
\widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hattautd) \approx \widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphitd) \cdot \frac{d}{d\phitd} g(\phitd)\Big|_{\phitd=\hatphitd}
\end{align}

\noindent where
\begin{align}\label{eq:lls_q_omega_}
    \hatqtd = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=2}^T x_{t-1}^2 \quad\text{and}\quad
    \hatomegatd = \sum_{\ell=-M}^M \left(1 - \frac{|\ell|}{M+1}\right) \quad \frac{1}{T} \sum_{1\le t - \ell \le T} (x_{t-1} \cdot \hat e_t^*)(x_{t-1-\ell} \cdot \hat e_{t-\ell}^*).
\end{align}

\noindent This estimator calculates a weighted sum of the regression scores $x_{t-1} \cdot \hat e_t^*$, where $\hat e_t^* = x_t - \hatphitd \cdot x_{t-1}$. The true $\omegatd$ is approximated by $\hatomegatd$ by taking a finite sum of the regression score covariances up to lag $M$, where $M$ is the lag-truncation (or bandwidth). The weights used in the sum decrease linearly with lag $\ell$, following a Bartlett kernel \citep{newey_simple_1987}. This kernel not only ensures the standard errors remain non-negative but also regularizes $\hatomegatd$ to change smoothly with $M$ \citep[Chapter~14.35]{hansen_econometrics_2022}.\\

\noindent For comparison we also include the naive estimator which simplifies under correct specification:
\begin{align}
    \widehat{\text{se}}_\text{Naive}(\hatphitd) &= \sqrt{\hatsigmatd^2 \hatqtd^{-1}},\\
    \widehat{\text{se}}_\text{Naive}(\hattautd) &\approx \widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{Naive}}(\hatphitd) \frac{d}{d\phitd} g(\phitd)\big|_{\phitd=\hatphitd},
\end{align}

\noindent where $\hatsigmatd^2 = 1/T \sum_{t=2}^T \hat e_t^{*2}$ is an estimate of the time-domain error variance.

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation-Domain Standard Error Estimator}\label{sec:stderr-autocorrelation-domain_}
The sample standard error estimator takes the form:
\begin{align}\label{eq:stderr-autocorrelation-domain_}
\widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphiad) = \sqrt{\hatqad^{-1}\;\hatomegaad\; \hatqad^{-1}}, \quad
\widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hattauad) \approx \widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{NW}}(\hatphiad) \cdot \frac{d}{d\phi} g(\phiad)\Big|_{\phiad=\hatphiad},
\end{align}

\noindent where
\begin{align}
    \hatqad &= \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=0}^K (\hat m_{{\phiad},k})^2 = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=0}^K (k {\hatphiad}^{k-1})^2,\\
    \hatomegaad &= \sum_{\ell=-M}^M \left(1 - \frac{|\ell|}{M+1}\right) \quad \frac{1}{K} \sum_{1 \le k - \ell \le K} (\hat m_{{\phiad}, k} \cdot \hat e_k^*) (\hat m_{{\phiad}, k-\ell} \cdot \hat e_{k-\ell}^*).\label{eq:nls_q_omega_}
\end{align}

\noindent This estimator calculates a weighted sum of the linearized regression scores $\hat m_{\phiad, k} \cdot \hat e_k^*$, where $\hat e_k^* = \hat\rho_k - (\hatphiad)^k$. The estimator $\hatomegaad$ takes a finite sum of these scores up to lag $M$, weighted by a Bartlett kernel, so that $\hatomegaad$ changes smoothly with $M$.\\

\noindent In the case of correct specification the equation simplifies to:
\begin{align}
    \widehat{\text{se}}_\text{Naive}(\hatphiad) &= \sqrt{\hatsigmaad^2 \hatqad^{-1}},\\
    \widehat{\text{se}}_\text{Naive}(\hattauad) &\approx \widehat{\text{se}}_{\text{Naive}}(\hatphiad) \frac{d}{d\phiad} g(\phiad)\Big|_{\phiad=\hatphiad},
\end{align}

\noindent where $\hatsigmaad^2 = 1/K \sum_{k=0}^K \hat e_k^{*2}$ is an estimate of the autocorrelation-domain error variance.\\

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation/Time-Domain Standard Error Estimator}\label{sec:stderr-autocorrelation/time-domain_}
The sample standard error for the hybrid method introduced in \nameref{sec:stderr-time-autocorrelation-domain} is equivalent to \eqref{eq:stderr-time-domain_}, except that the time-domain estimator $\hatphitd$ is replaced with the autocorrelation-domain estimator $\hatphiad$, redefining the errors in the time domain as $\hat e_t^* = x_t - \hatphiad \cdot x_{t-1}$. This substitution applies the time-domain variance estimator to the sampling variability of the autocorrelation-domain parameter, needed when applying these timescale methods to autoregressive processes as \eqref{eq:stderr-autocorrelation-domain_} will underestimate this case.
